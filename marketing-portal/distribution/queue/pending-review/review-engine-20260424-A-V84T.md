# SECTION 1: THE 5 MOMENT PACKAGES

## MOMENT 1 — After the Orientation Session

**Why this moment:**

The orientation session eliminates the fear that kept this person out of a gym for months — sometimes years. They walked in not knowing what to expect, bracing for embarrassment, for equipment they didn't understand, for staff too busy or too polished to help someone at their starting point. Thirty minutes later, they know the layout, they have a plan, and they've already spoken to someone who treated them like they belonged there. The relief is real and it's immediate. That emotional state — relief plus anticipation plus the absence of the fear they'd been carrying — is the single highest-positive-emotion moment in their first 30 days. When someone asks them to share how they feel right now, they are not performing a review. They are releasing something they genuinely want to say.

This is also the most structurally reliable trigger in the entire system. Every new member has an orientation. The volume is guaranteed. The consistency of the emotional state is high. The review written at this moment tends to include the phrase "I was nervous about coming in" — which is exactly the language a skeptical prospect needs to read.

---

**IN-PERSON SCRIPT**

*Context: Orientation just ended. Coach or staff member is walking the member toward the door. The session went well — which is the baseline expectation. This is not for difficult sessions.*

---

> "Completely fine — I'll send it over in a few minutes. It's just one tap."

---

**WHY THIS SCRIPT WORKS:**

The first sentence does two things: it names their emotional experience ("what most people spend months being scared of") without making it awkward, and it signals that what they just felt is worth sharing. It doesn't open with "can you leave us a review" — it arrives at the ask through a genuine reflection on what just happened. The word "review" doesn't appear at all. The phrase "share that" is the natural language of someone telling a friend about an experience, not the transactional language of a business requesting a rating.

The phone handoff is non-negotiable. The member who walks out intending to find the gym on Google and leave a review later will not do it. Not because they don't mean to — because friction defeats intention every time. The link goes directly to the review input page. One tap. That is the difference between 8% follow-through and 35% follow-through.

## MOMENT 2 — After First Month Completion

**Why this moment:**

Thirty days. For the lifestyle member, this is the number they've never hit. They've joined gyms before and been gone by week three. The people who matter to them — their partner, their friends — have watched them start and stop enough times that nobody says "good for you" anymore when they join something new. But thirty days is real. Thirty days is different. The physical changes are just becoming perceptible: energy in the afternoon, sleeping through the night, clothes that fit slightly differently. They haven't lost twenty pounds. They've done something harder — they've changed a pattern that had been defeating them for years.

The review written at this moment will not say "great equipment." It will say "I've tried gyms four times in the last decade and never made it past the first month." That sentence, from a real person with their name attached, is worth more to a hesitant prospect than any ad you can write.

## MOMENT 3 — After a Positive Check-in Response

**Why this moment:**

A member who responds to a routine check-in with something real — "slept better than I have in months," "kept up with my son at the park this weekend," "finally don't dread Mondays" — has already done the emotional work of articulating what changed. They are not being asked to construct a review from scratch. They are being asked to say the thing they just said to you, in a place where other people can hear it.

This trigger requires a human on the other end. The member opened a personal channel with a real response. An automated reply to that message — even a warm one — reads as automation and the moment collapses. A real staff member responds, receives what they said, and then makes the ask. The whole exchange should feel like a conversation, not a funnel.

## MOMENT 4 — After Evolt or Progress Scan Shows Positive Results

**Why this moment:**

The scan moment is when "I think I feel better" becomes "I can see it." The subjectivity is gone. The member has data. They walked in four or six or eight weeks ago with a baseline number, and today's number tells a specific story. The pride is intense and it is specific in a way that daily life hadn't let it be — because daily life doesn't send you a readout.

The review written at this moment tends to include measurable language the member chooses themselves. These reviews convert the analytically-minded prospect who wants to know if the program actually works — not just whether the coaches are friendly.

---

**[COMPLIANCE FLAG — FTC / META]:** The in-person script and SMS at this trigger do not direct the member toward specific body composition metrics in their review. The ask references "what you saw today" and "something real to share" — the member decides what to include. AHRI does not script the review's content. Prompting members to share specific weight, body fat percentage, or muscle mass numbers in their review would constitute an implied result claim requiring substantiation and typical-result disclosure. The member's autonomy in choosing what to share keeps the review authentic and legally clean.

## MOMENT 5 — Anniversary Milestones

**Why this moment:**

The 3-month member beat the window when most people stop. The 6-month member is no longer trying a gym — they are a person who goes to the gym. The 12-month member has a story that no recent joiner can tell. Their review is not an evaluation of a first impression. It is a report from the other side of the thing that every hesitant prospect is afraid they can't do.

Reviews from members with sustained tenure are qualitatively different from early reviews. A prospect reading a 12-month member's review is reading proof that the habit is sustainable — which is the single deepest fear the lifestyle member carries. "What if I try and quit again?" The 12-month review is the answer to that question from someone who had the same fear.

# SECTION 2: THE 8 RESPONSE TEMPLATES

---

## Template 1 — 5-Star Enthusiastic (Detailed Review)

**When to use:** Reviewer wrote multiple sentences. Referenced a specific staff member, a specific moment, or a specific outcome. The review has real substance.

**Personalization note:** Read the review before responding. Pull one specific detail they mentioned — a name, a moment, a feeling — and include it. A response that references what they actually wrote is read by prospects as proof that real people are paying attention here.

---

**Variation A:**

> [First Name] — thank you for taking the time to write this. What you described about [specific element from review] is exactly what we're here for. We're really glad you're part of this. See you [on Tuesday / next week / soon].

**Variation B:**

> This is wonderful to read — thank you. [Specific element from review] says it better than we ever could in an ad. We're proud to have you here, and we'll make sure [staff member name if mentioned] sees this.

**Variation C:**

> Thank you for this. Really. [Specific element from their review] — that's the thing that gets said in the building, but it means something different to see it written down. We're grateful you're here.

*[Under 75 words each. Personalize by replacing bracketed elements with specifics from the actual review.]*

---

## Template 2 — 5-Star Brief (One-Liner)

**When to use:** Reviewer gave 5 stars with minimal text — "Great gym!" / "Love it!" / "Highly recommend."

**Personalization note:** You can't reference their specific experience because they didn't describe one. Instead, make them feel seen for even a brief review — and say something real about what the gym values, without it sounding like a mission statement.

---

> Thank you, [First Name] — this made our day. We care a lot about making sure every person who walks in here feels like they belong. Really glad that's coming through. Hope to see you soon.

*[Under 50 words. Insert first name from review. If no first name is available, remove the name and start with "Thank you"]*

---

## Template 3 — 4-Star (Good Experience, Implied Gap)

**When to use:** Reviewer gave 4 stars. Something was good — but something wasn't quite right. They may or may not name the specific issue.

**Approach:** Don't treat 4 stars as a failure that needs defending. Treat it as a member who is mostly happy and deserves a gracious response — plus a genuine invitation to tell you what the 5th star would have required.

---

> [First Name], thank you — we're really glad [specific positive element from review] landed the way it did. Four stars from someone who's paying attention means something to us, and we'd love to understand what a fifth would have looked like. Can we reach out directly? Your honest read helps us more than you might think.

*[Under 75 words. Always close with an invitation to talk offline. Never try to resolve the implied gap in a public response — that conversation belongs in private.]*

---

## Template 4 — 3-Star or Below (Critical Review)

**When to use:** Reviewer gave 1, 2, or 3 stars and described a genuinely negative experience.

**The most important template in this document.** Prospects read negative reviews and then read the response. The response is the audition. A gym that responds with grace and accountability to a critical review signals to every future prospect: if something goes wrong here, they will make it right. That is a trust signal that no amount of 5-star reviews can produce on its own.

**What this template never does:**
- Begins with "We're sorry you feel that way" (invalidates their experience)
- Begins with a public factual correction (creates a public argument — never the right venue)
- Is defensive about the staff member, policy, or decision referenced in the review
- Goes over 100 words

---

> [First Name], thank you for sharing this — I'm sorry your experience here didn't match what we want to provide. What you described isn't what we want anyone to feel when they come in, and I'd genuinely like to understand what happened and make it right. Can I reach out to you directly? My name is [Owner/Manager name] and I'm here: [phone or email]. Thank you for taking the time.

*[Under 100 words. Respond within 24 hours. Move the conversation offline immediately. Do not under any circumstances engage in a point-by-point rebuttal in the public reply. After the public response, follow up by phone or email if contact information is available.]*

**Strategic note for Kai:** A well-handled critical review, followed by a genuine private resolution, occasionally results in the reviewer updating their rating voluntarily. Never ask them to change the review. Handle the issue completely. Some will update it because that is what a fair person does when a business makes things right.

---

## Template 5 — Fake or Spam Review

**When to use:** The review clearly comes from someone who was not a member — wrong business name, no specific details, generic attack language, reviewing from a profile with no history, or pattern consistent with coordinated spam.

**Approach:** Professional, calm, factual. No accusation. No defensiveness. Simply note the anomaly for other readers and signal that you stand behind your actual members' experiences.

---

> Thank you for the rating. We've reviewed our records and we don't have any record of a visit or membership under this name. If we've made an error or there's been any confusion, please reach out directly — [phone or email] — and we'll make it right. We take all feedback seriously.

*[Under 50 words. After posting, flag the review for removal through Google Business Profile manager immediately. Document: date of review, content, date of response, date of removal flag. If multiple fake reviews appear in a short window, escalate to Kai — this may indicate a coordinated reputation attack from a competitor.]*

---

## Template 6 — Staff-Specific Praise

**When to use:** Reviewer specifically named and praised a staff member or coach.

**Approach:** Acknowledge the member's experience. Celebrate the staff member publicly — this matters for staff morale and signals to prospects that the team is real and recognized.

---

> [First Name], this means a lot — thank you. [Staff member name] is exactly the reason we built this place the way we did, and we're going to make sure they see this. We're really glad you're here.

*[Under 50 words. Share the review with the named staff member directly. This is a retention and culture investment, not just a marketing response.]*

---

## Template 7 — New Member's First Review (Early Tenure)

**When to use:** The reviewer mentions they just joined, just completed their first week or two, or explicitly notes they are new. The review is positive but early.

**Approach:** Warmly acknowledge where they are. Add one forward-looking line that reinforces they are in the right place — and deepens their commitment.

---

> [First Name] — welcome. Really. The first few weeks are the ones that set everything up, and we're glad they've felt like this. We're looking forward to being part of what comes next for you.

*[Under 50 words. The phrase "what comes next" activates the forward identity — reinforces the consistency principle and plants the seed of continued tenure.]*

---

## Template 8 — Long-Tenured Member (6+ Months)

**When to use:** The reviewer mentions how long they've been a member — 6 months, a year, "years." Their review carries weight and they clearly know it.

**Approach:** Acknowledge the tenure directly. Make them feel seen for the full arc of what they've built here — not just the fact that they left a review.

---

> [First Name] — [X months / X years]. You've built something real here. Thank you for sharing it, and thank you for sticking around long enough to have something worth sharing. We're proud to have you here.

*[Under 50 words. Reference their specific tenure from the review if they mentioned it.]*
