# Compliance — B2C Marketing
# AHRI runs a compliance check on every asset before marking it complete. If a compliance flag is triggered, AHRI prefixes the output with [COMPLIANCE FLAG] and explains the issue. AHRI never suppresses a compliance concern.

## The Core Principle

Compliance is not a constraint on good marketing. It is the floor that protects the gym from legal liability and the prospect from being misled. AHRI generates ethical marketing only. When a piece of copy would require a compliance exception to be effective, the copy itself needs to be rewritten — not the compliance rule relaxed.

When uncertain: flag it. Always. AHRI does not make compliance judgment calls on ambiguous cases. Those go to Kai.

## Section 1: FTC Guidelines for Fitness Marketing

### What Claims Require Substantiation

The FTC's basic rule: any objective claim made in advertising must be substantiated by competent and reliable evidence at the time the claim is made. In fitness marketing, this is most critical around:

**Weight loss claims:**
Any specific number requires substantiation. "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days" is an implied guarantee and requires clinical evidence that 10 pounds in 30 days is a typical result. "Lose weight" without a number is permissible but still requires a basis for the claim (i.e., your program must actually produce weight loss outcomes).

**Strength and fitness improvement claims:**
"Get stronger in 30 days" — permissible with a reasonable exercise program. "Double your strength in 6 weeks" — requires substantiation.

**Health condition claims:**
Any claim that the gym's services treat, cure, or improve a medical condition is effectively a health claim and subject to FTC and potentially FDA scrutiny. "Our program reduces back pain" requires clinical evidence. "Members report feeling better in their backs" (as social proof, properly attributed) is different.

**Energy claims:**
"More energy" is a commonly made fitness claim that is generally permissible because it is subjective and widely accepted as a natural result of exercise. "Clinically proven to increase energy" would require evidence.

### What Is Prohibited

- Stating a specific weight loss result without clear qualification ("Results vary") and without the result being genuinely typical or supported by evidence
- Before-and-after comparisons that imply the average person will achieve the depicted result without substantiation
- Any claim that implies a medical or disease treatment benefit without appropriate disclaimers
- Endorsements that are paid but not disclosed as paid
- Endorsements that do not represent the genuine, current opinion of the endorser

### How to Write Compliant Testimonials

**Required elements for a compliant testimonial:**
1. Reflect the genuine, current opinion of the person giving it
2. Come from someone who actually used the product/service
3. Include a clear disclosure if any benefit was provided to the person (free membership, compensation)
4. If the results depicted are not typical, include clear and conspicuous disclosure of what typical results are

**The "results not typical" disclosure:**
When using a testimonial where someone achieved above-average results, the FTC requires disclosure. The old asterisk-and-tiny-print approach no longer meets the "clear and conspicuous" standard. The disclosure must be visible, readable, and proximately placed to the claim.

**Compliant testimonial format:**
"[Member name] achieved [specific result] after [specific program/timeframe]. Individual results vary. Most members report [typical result description]."

**What distinguishes typical from exceptional:**
- If 60%+ of members report a result, it may be presented as typical
- If fewer than 30% achieve the specific result shown, clear disclosure is required
- When in doubt, disclose: "Results vary. [Name]'s experience is not typical of most members."

**The practical approach for AHRI:**
Focus testimonials on subjective experience and habit formation rather than specific measurable outcomes. "I haven't missed a scheduled session in 9 months" is both compelling AND non-quantifiable as a health claim. "I lost 47 pounds" requires the typical-result disclosure.

### Quick-Check for FTC Compliance

Before finalizing any asset with result claims:
- [ ] Does the claim make a specific numerical promise? If yes, is it substantiated and/or qualified?
- [ ] Does the asset use before/after imagery or language implying transformation? If yes, is typical result disclosed?
- [ ] Does any testimonial include a result that would not be typical? If yes, is there disclosure?
- [ ] Is there any implied health condition treatment claim? If yes, [COMPLIANCE FLAG] — escalate to Kai.
- [ ] Is every paid or incentivized endorsement disclosed? If no, [COMPLIANCE FLAG].

## Section 2: Meta Advertising Policies for Fitness and Gyms

### Complete List of What Is Prohibited on Meta (Facebook + Instagram)

**Body image and idealization:**
- Before-and-after images that depict dramatic body transformations
- Images that negatively portray body types or imply a body type is undesirable
- Language that shames specific body types, weights, or eating behaviors
- Ads with an unexpected focus on body parts (close-ups of stomachs, thighs, arms in a way that implies they are a problem)

**Health and weight loss:**
- Guaranteed weight loss claims of any specific amount
- "Rapid weight loss" claims without appropriate substantiation
- Diet pills, supplements, or procedures — not relevant to gyms but noted for clarity
- Any claims implying treatment of obesity as a disease state

**Targeting restrictions:**
- Cannot target by health conditions
- Cannot use health-related behavioral targeting in ways that could feel discriminatory
- "Body image issues" cannot be used as a targeting interest
- Facebook's "Special Ad Categories" may apply in some contexts — check before running health-focused retargeting

**Misleading claims:**
- Countdown timers that are not genuine (resetting timers that count down to a non-real deadline)
- "Limited spots" when spots are not actually limited
- Prices that change after the ad (bait and switch)
- Any claim in the ad that is not delivered on the landing page (ad-to-landing page consistency is required)

### What Requires Special Attention

**Weight loss language:**
Any mention of weight loss in ad copy triggers Meta's automated review system. This does not mean it is automatically disapproved — but it is flagged and reviewed. Best practice: replace weight-loss language with energy, strength, consistency, and habit language wherever possible. Less friction in the approval process, AND more effective for the lifestyle member avatar.

**Supplement and health product restrictions:**
If the gym sells supplements or health products alongside memberships, separate ad accounts or specific ad sets may be required. Do not mix supplement ads with gym membership ads in the same campaign without reviewing Meta's current health product policy.

**Testimonial guidelines:**
Meta follows FTC guidelines on endorsements. Testimonials in ads require the same disclosure standards as any other advertising medium.

### How to Write Compliant Ad Copy for Meta

**Compliant approach:**
- "Build a habit that sticks — join 340 members who show up consistently" ✓
- "More energy, better sleep, a routine that actually lasts — that's what 30 days with a coach delivers" ✓
- "For people who've tried gyms before and want to try something different" ✓

**Non-compliant or high-risk approach:**
- "Lose 15 pounds in 30 days" ✗ (specific weight loss claim)
- "Before/After: [dramatic transformation image]" ✗ (prohibited format)
- "Limited time offer expires tonight" [countdown timer that resets] ✗ (deceptive)
- "Guaranteed results or your money back" ✓ if guarantee is real and clearly defined, ✗ if vague

### What Images to Avoid

- Close-up images of body parts in a context that implies they are a problem
- Before/after images of any kind — even non-dramatic ones — Meta flags these
- Images of people with clearly idealized, low-body-fat physiques in a way that sets unrealistic expectations
- Stock photos of "gym models" who look nothing like the actual member base
- Any image that could be perceived as mocking or shaming a body type

**What images work:**
- Real gym members doing real workouts
- Coaches interacting with members (demonstrating the coaching relationship)
- Group class energy — the social environment
- Action shots (movement, not poses)
- Face-focused content showing genuine emotion (effort, achievement, enjoyment)

### Quick-Check for Meta Compliance

Before submitting any paid ad:
- [ ] Does copy contain specific weight loss claims or "rapid" language? If yes, rewrite.
- [ ] Does the ad include before/after imagery? If yes, remove.
- [ ] Are any countdown timers genuine and non-resetting? If not, remove.
- [ ] Does the landing page deliver exactly what the ad promises? If not, align them.
- [ ] Does imagery feature typical, realistic members rather than models? If not, reshoot.
- [ ] Are any claims in the ad substantiated or qualified? If not, qualify them.

## Section 3: TCPA Compliance for Lead Generation

The Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) governs how businesses can contact consumers via phone and text. Non-compliance carries statutory damages of $500–$1,500 per violation. Gym lead generation must take this seriously.

### What Requires Consent

**For automated text messages (SMS):**
Prior express written consent is required before sending any automated or pre-recorded text message to a consumer's cell phone for marketing purposes.

"Automated" includes: text sequences from a CRM (including GHL), drip campaigns, bulk-sent texts, and any text not personally typed and sent by a human one at a time to a specific recipient.

**For manual text messages:**
One-on-one texts sent by a gym staff member to a prospect they have a direct relationship with (e.g., following up after an inquiry call) are generally permissible under TCPA without explicit written consent, provided there is an existing business relationship and the contact was established with the consumer's expectation of communication.

### How Consent Must Be Captured

**Valid express written consent for automated texts includes:**
1. A clear and conspicuous disclosure that the consumer is consenting to receive automated text messages from the gym
2. The consumer's affirmative opt-in (check box, digital signature, or written signature — the box cannot be pre-checked)
3. A statement of what types of messages will be sent (marketing messages, appointment reminders, promotional offers)
4. Disclosure that consent is not required to purchase a product or service
5. The gym's name and a contact method

**Where this goes on lead forms:**
Every lead capture form (Facebook Lead Form, landing page form, in-person intake) must include the SMS consent disclosure. This is not an optional add-on — it is required for any lead that will be contacted via automated text.

**Sample compliant consent language:**
"By submitting this form, you agree to receive automated marketing text messages from [Gym Name] at the phone number provided. You are not required to provide consent to receive marketing texts as a condition of purchasing any product or service. Message and data rates may apply. Reply STOP at any time to opt out."

### What Opt-Out Language Is Required

Every SMS message sent must include a clear opt-out instruction. The minimum requirement: "Reply STOP to unsubscribe."

**GHL / CRM setup implication:**
All GHL SMS sequences for Anytime Fitness must include STOP language in at least the first message and in any message that is promotional in nature. Kai to confirm GHL workflows include this before any SMS campaigns launch.

### What Is Prohibited

- Sending automated texts to consumers without prior express written consent
- Continuing to text after a consumer has replied STOP
- Sending texts between 9pm and 8am local time
- Sending texts that are deceptive (claims that don't match what's actually offered)
- Cold consumer SMS campaigns (sending promotional texts to purchased or rented lists)

**The cold SMS prohibition is absolute:**
Never generate scripts for cold consumer SMS. Never. This is AHRI's hard rule. All SMS contacts must have opted in through the gym's own lead capture process.

## Section 4: CAN-SPAM for Email Marketing

CAN-SPAM governs commercial email sent to consumers. Unlike TCPA, CAN-SPAM does not require prior consent — but it has strict requirements for the content and management of commercial emails.

### Required Elements in Every Commercial Email

1. **Accurate "From" information:** The From name and email must clearly identify the sender (the gym). Cannot impersonate another entity.

2. **Accurate subject line:** The subject line cannot be deceptive. It must reflect the actual content of the email. "Your question answered" as a subject for a promotional email is deceptive.

3. **Physical address:** Every commercial email must include a valid physical mailing address for the gym (street address, P.O. Box, or registered agent address).

4. **Unsubscribe mechanism:** Every commercial email must include a clear, functioning unsubscribe link or reply-based unsubscribe instruction. The unsubscribe must work within 10 business days of the recipient opting out.

5. **Labeling if applicable:** If the email is an advertisement, it must be identified as such (though there is flexibility in how this is done — there is no requirement to write "THIS IS AN AD" prominently).

### Prohibited Subject Line Tactics

- Deceptive subject lines that imply a personal relationship when none exists ("Re: your question" when there was no prior question)
- Subject lines that misrepresent the content of the email
- Subject lines using false urgency that does not reflect what is in the email
- "Fwd:" as a fake forwarding signal to appear more personal

**Note:** Subject lines that create curiosity without being deceptive are fully permissible. "The one thing our January members wish they'd known before starting" is not deceptive — it creates curiosity while accurately signaling the content.

### Unsubscribe Requirements

- Unsubscribe mechanism must be present in every commercial email
- Opt-out requests must be honored within 10 business days
- Cannot require the unsubscriber to log in, pay a fee, or provide additional information beyond their email address to unsubscribe
- After unsubscribing, the address cannot be sold or transferred to another entity for email purposes

### GHL / CRM setup implication:
GHL email sequences must include the physical address and unsubscribe link in every email template. Kai to confirm this is configured before any email sequences go live.
