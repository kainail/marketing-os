# Miller — StoryBrand (Building a StoryBrand)
# Source: Donald Miller, Building a StoryBrand. AHRI applies this to every piece of copy: ads, landing pages, emails, flyers, social content.

---

## The Core Insight

Human brains are wired for story. A gym that tells a clear story about its member — not about itself — wins attention, trust, and conversion. Most gym marketing fails because it makes the gym the hero. The gym talks about its equipment, its coaches, its years of experience, its community. The prospect's brain hears: "this is not about me."

The member is the hero. Always. The gym is the guide. The guide never saves the day — the guide equips the hero to save their own day.

When you put the member at the center of the story, marketing stops feeling like advertising and starts feeling like a conversation between a person who has a problem and someone who can help them solve it.

## The SB7 Framework — Applied to Gyms

### Element 1: A Character (The Hero)

Every story needs a hero. In gym marketing, that hero is always the member — never the gym, never the coach, never the brand.

**The character has a want.** Not a need — a want. The want is specific, achievable, and emotionally resonant. "Get healthy" is a category, not a want. "Have enough energy to be present with my kids after work" is a want.

**For the lifestyle member avatar, the wants are:**
- To feel like themselves again — the person they were 10 years ago
- To have energy that lasts through the afternoon
- To stop feeling self-conscious in their body
- To be able to do physical things without thinking about whether they can
- To feel proud of themselves for doing something they said they would do

**What AHRI does:** Before writing any asset, articulate the specific want for the active avatar. Write it in first person as the avatar would say it. Every word of the asset then serves that want.

---

### Element 2: Has a Problem

Stories are not about wants. Stories are about problems. The problem is what stands between the hero and their want. Miller identifies three layers of problem: external, internal, and philosophical.

**The External Problem** is the surface-level, tangible obstacle:
- "I don't have a gym membership"
- "I don't know how to work out"
- "I don't have time to go to the gym consistently"
- "I've tried before and stopped going"

**The Internal Problem** is the emotional experience the external problem creates:
- "I feel like I've let myself go"
- "I feel embarrassed about where I am physically"
- "I don't trust myself to follow through"
- "I feel like I'm watching my energy and confidence slip away"

**The Philosophical Problem** is the deeper, worldview-level injustice:
- "I shouldn't have to choose between my career, my family, and my health"
- "People deserve to feel good in their bodies regardless of their schedule"
- "It's wrong that most gyms are designed for people who are already fit"

**The failure of most gym marketing:** It addresses only the external problem. It talks about equipment (the solution to "I don't know how to work out") and schedules (the solution to "I don't have time"). It never touches the internal problem — the shame, the self-doubt, the history of quitting — which is what the prospect is actually living inside.

**The most persuasive gym copy** acknowledges all three layers: names the external obstacle briefly, dwells in the internal emotion (because that's where the prospect lives), and resolves at the philosophical level (because that's what makes them feel understood as a whole human being, not just a fitness prospect).

---

### Element 3: And Meets a Guide

The guide is the gym. But notice the framing — the guide does not appear until the hero has been established and their problem has been named. The gym is not the subject of the story. It enters as a supporting character.

The guide has two essential characteristics:

**1. Empathy:** The guide demonstrates that it understands the hero's problem from the inside. Not clinical understanding — emotional understanding. "We know what it feels like to start something and stop. We've had that conversation with hundreds of people. We built this program because of those conversations."

**2. Authority:** The guide has the credibility to actually help. Not bragging — evidence. "Our coaches have helped 340 members build consistent habits in the last 3 years. Here are three of their stories."

**The order matters:** Empathy first. Authority second. A guide who opens with authority without empathy sounds like they're selling. A guide who opens with empathy sounds like they understand — which is what earns the right to then demonstrate authority.

**What gym marketing gets wrong about the guide:**
- Leading with "We've been in business for 10 years" — that's authority without empathy. The prospect doesn't care about your tenure until they feel understood.
- Listing coach certifications before acknowledging the emotional reality the prospect is living — credentials don't convert until trust is established.
- Making the gym the main character of testimonial posts: "We're so proud of what our member achieved" — the gym made itself the hero of its own success story.

---

### Element 4: Who Gives Them a Plan

The hero will not move without a clear, simple plan. Ambiguity is the enemy of action. Most gym marketing asks prospects to "contact us" or "learn more" without showing them what the path looks like.

The plan removes the risk of taking the first step. It answers: "What exactly will happen if I do this?"

**Two types of plans:**

**The Process Plan** — shows the steps:
1. Book a free 20-minute intro visit
2. Meet your coach and tour the gym
3. Start your first session (we'll walk you through everything)

This plan is so simple that each step is non-threatening. The prospect does not need to commit to a membership to take Step 1. They just need to book a visit. That is the only ask.

**The Agreement Plan** — addresses the values the prospect is worried about violating:
- "No judgment — everyone here was a beginner"
- "No long contracts — month-to-month, cancel anytime"
- "No pressure — your coach works at your pace, not the class average"

Both plans serve the same purpose: they make it safe to take the first step.

**AHRI principle:** Every piece of conversion copy must include a clear 3-step process plan. If a landing page does not show the path, it will not convert. If an email does not tell the reader exactly what the next step is, they will do nothing.

---

### Element 5: And Calls Them to Action

The hero will not act unless they are asked to act. Clearly. Directly.

Miller identifies two types of calls to action:

**Direct CTA:** A specific request for a specific action.
- "Book your free intro visit — [button]"
- "Text START to [number]"
- "Claim your $1 first month — [button]"
- "Call [name] at [number] today"

**Transitional CTA:** A lower-commitment step for prospects not yet ready for the direct CTA.
- "Download our free beginner's guide"
- "Watch the 4-minute video about our new member program"
- "Read what our members say"

**Which to use when:**
- Level 4–5 awareness: direct CTA is appropriate — these prospects are ready
- Level 2–3 awareness: transitional CTA first, then retarget with direct CTA

**The failure mode:** Timid CTAs. "Feel free to reach out if you'd like." "We'd love to hear from you." These are not calls to action. They are invitations that carry no urgency and no specificity. The prospect's default is inaction. Your CTA must overcome that default with clarity and specificity.

**AHRI rule:** Every piece of conversion copy has one primary CTA. Not two. Not "call or email or click or DM us." One. The multiplicity of options reduces action.

### Element 6: That Helps Them Avoid Failure

Stories need stakes. Without a failure state, there is no urgency. What does the hero's life look like if they do not solve this problem?

This is not fear-mongering. It is honest story. The lifestyle member's life without a solution is: the drift continues, the energy stays low, the self-consciousness deepens, the things they stopped doing stay stopped. That is real. It is not manipulative to name it — it is empathetic.

**How to use failure in gym copy without using shame:**

Shame: "If you don't do something about this, you'll keep feeling awful and it will only get worse."
(Attacks the person. Judges. Creates defensiveness.)

Honest stakes: "The thing about drift is that it doesn't stop on its own. Without something to interrupt the pattern, next January looks like this January."
(Names the consequence without attacking the person. Creates urgency without shame.)

**Examples for the lifestyle member:**

"Every month you wait is another month of the Sunday night feeling — the one where you wish things were different but Monday starts the same cycle."

"The alternatives to acting are not neutral. They just feel that way in the short term."

"Three years from now, either you built a habit that compounds, or you spent three more years meaning to."

**The rule:** Use failure to create urgency, not shame. The prospect already feels some version of this. Your job is to name it honestly, briefly, and then immediately show the path forward. Never dwell in the failure state. Name it and move past it.

---

### Element 7: And Ends in Success

The success vision is the full articulation of the dream outcome. What does the hero's life look like when they win?

This is the most important element to make vivid and specific. Generic success ("you'll feel great!") has zero power. Specific success ("you'll be the one chasing your kid at the park instead of watching from the bench") creates the emotional pull that makes people act.

**The success vision must be:**
1. Specific enough to be pictured
2. Rooted in life outcomes, not fitness metrics
3. Authentic to what this specific gym actually delivers
4. Written in the avatar's voice, not marketing voice

**Success vision examples for the lifestyle member:**

"You're the person who shows up at work on Monday morning and actually has the energy to care about the week."

"You say yes to the hike. To the beach trip. To the photo. Because you're not hiding anymore."

"Your doctor does a double-take at your annual checkup. And you get to feel proud instead of embarrassed."

"You wake up on a Tuesday and realize you've been to the gym 4 times this week. Not because you had to. Because you wanted to."

"Six months from now, the version of you looking back at today is grateful you made this call."

## Why the Member Must Be the Hero and the Gym Must Be the Guide

When the gym becomes the hero, something specific happens in the prospect's brain: the gym becomes competition.

When a gym's marketing is about how great the gym is — its equipment, its coaches, its results, its brand — the prospect unconsciously asks: "What does this have to do with me?" The gym has made itself the subject. The prospect is a bystander. Bystanders do not buy.

When the member is the hero, the gym's greatness is framed through what it enables the member to do. The equipment enables the member's transformation. The coaches enable the member's success. The community enables the member's consistency. Everything the gym is and has becomes relevant because it serves the hero.

**What happens when gyms reverse the roles (real examples):**

Wrong: "We have the best coaches in [city]."
Right: "You'll have a coach who texts you twice a week — because they know exactly when people stop, and they're not going to let that happen to you."

Wrong: "Our facility is state-of-the-art."
Right: "Everything you need to build a consistent habit is here. You don't need to figure it out. We've already figured it out."

Wrong: "We've helped hundreds of members achieve their goals."
Right: "The 340 people who joined in the last three years didn't succeed because they found more motivation. They succeeded because we built a system that makes quitting harder than continuing."

The information is the same. The framing is the difference between a brand talking about itself and a guide speaking to a hero.

## The One-Liner Formula

**"We help [avatar] [verb] [outcome] so they can [identity/life goal]."**

The one-liner is not a tagline. It is a precision tool that forces clarity on who you serve, what you do, and what the member's life looks like after. Every person on the gym's staff should be able to say this when someone asks "what does your gym do?"

**Three gym one-liner examples:**

1. "We help adults who've tried gyms before and quit build a consistent workout habit in 30 days — so they stop starting over and start building on something real."

2. "We help busy professionals over 40 build strength and energy in three sessions a week — so they show up fully for the things that matter outside the gym."

3. "We help people who feel out of shape and embarrassed about it take their first step back into fitness — in an environment where they'll never feel judged."

**How to build a gym's one-liner:**
1. Who is the specific avatar? (Not "everyone" — the primary avatar)
2. What is the specific problem you solve? (Name the external or internal problem)
3. What does their life look like after? (Not fitness outcome — life outcome)

## How to Write a BrandScript for a Gym — Complete Template

The BrandScript is the full StoryBrand exercise applied to one gym. It becomes the source document for all copy.

**BrandScript Template — Anytime Fitness:**

**Character:** Adults 30–55 who feel like they've lost their energy, their fitness baseline, and their confidence about their body. They've tried gyms before. They want to feel like themselves again.

**External Problem:** They don't have a consistent workout habit. They don't know where to start. They've joined gyms before and stopped going.

**Internal Problem:** They feel embarrassed, self-conscious, and like they've "let themselves go." They don't trust themselves to follow through. They're afraid of failing again.

**Philosophical Problem:** People who work hard and take care of everyone else deserve to take care of themselves too. It's wrong that most fitness systems are designed for people who are already fit.

**Guide — Empathy Statement:** "We've had this conversation hundreds of times. The person who feels like they should have started sooner. Who's worried they'll quit again. We built our new member program because of those conversations — not despite them."

**Guide — Authority Statement:** "[X] members in [location] have built consistent habits through our program. 47 reviews. 4.9 stars. Our approach is different: we don't just give you equipment. We give you a system."

**Plan:**
1. Book a free 20-minute intro visit — no commitment, no pitch
2. Start your first coached week with a done-for-you plan
3. Build the habit with twice-weekly coach check-ins for your first 30 days

**Direct CTA:** "Book your free visit"

**Transitional CTA:** "Download the New Member Kickstart Guide"

**Failure:** "Without a system, January looks like last January. The pattern doesn't break itself."

**Success:** "Six months from now: more energy, more confidence, and — for the first time — the feeling that you actually kept a promise to yourself."

## How StoryBrand Applies to Each Asset Type

**Ads (paid and organic):**
Lead with the hero's problem (internal) or the success vision. The guide appears as the implied source of the solution. Plan is simplified to one step. CTA is direct.

**Landing pages:**
Full SB7 arc. Hero + problem above the fold. Guide credentials mid-page. Plan in 3 steps. Testimonials (success stories). Direct CTA 2–3 times. Failure stakes near the CTA to create urgency.

**Emails:**
Subject line = hero's problem or success vision. Body = abbreviated story arc. One CTA only. Keep short — the email earns the click, the landing page earns the conversion.

**Social content:**
Most social content covers one element of the SB7 arc. A post about a member milestone is the Success element. A post about starting over is the Problem + Empathy elements. A post about the accountability system is the Plan. Content does not need to be the full arc — it serves the arc over time.

**Flyers:**
Headline = hero's want or problem. Sub-headline = guide's empathy. Bullet points = simplified plan. CTA = single action. Failure stakes omitted (too short). Success vision in one line.

## Combining StoryBrand With Schwartz Awareness Levels

| Awareness Level | Which SB7 Elements to Lead With |
|---|---|
| Level 1 (Unaware) | Character (hero's life) + hint of external problem. No guide. No plan. No CTA. |
| Level 2 (Problem Aware) | Character + external problem + internal problem. Empathy from guide. No plan yet. Transitional CTA at most. |
| Level 3 (Solution Aware) | Internal problem + guide (empathy first, then authority) + abbreviated plan. Soft CTA. |
| Level 4 (Product Aware) | Guide (authority) + plan + success vision + direct CTA. Failure stakes near CTA. |
| Level 5 (Most Aware) | Plan + direct CTA + failure stakes (brief) + success vision. Offer specifics. |

**The principle:** Cold audiences need more story. Hot audiences need more plan. The awareness level dictates how much of the narrative you complete before asking for action.

## What a Gym Offers (Features) vs. What the Member Wants (Identity Transformation)

The deepest StoryBrand insight for gym marketing: members do not want a gym. They want an identity.

They want to be the kind of person who works out. They want to be the kind of person who kept a promise to themselves. They want to be the kind of person their kids talk about and say "my mom/dad is fit." The gym is a mechanism for becoming that person.

| What the Gym Offers (Features) | What the Member Wants (Identity) |
|---|---|
| 24/7 access | Freedom and control over their own schedule |
| Certified coaches | Confidence that they will not fail this time |
| A clean, modern facility | A place they feel good walking into — not intimidated |
| A variety of equipment | Enough to do what they need without thinking |
| Group classes | Belonging — a reason to show up that isn't just willpower |
| Personal training | Someone who sees them and holds them accountable |

**AHRI application:** Never lead with the feature. Lead with the identity outcome. Then connect the feature to the outcome as the mechanism. "24/7 access — because your schedule shouldn't be the reason you miss a session" is infinitely stronger than "24/7 access — open when you need us."
