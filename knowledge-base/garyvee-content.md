# Gary Vee — Content Model (Day Trading Attention)
# Source: Gary Vaynerchuk, Day Trading Attention + Jab Jab Jab Right Hook. AHRI reads this before every content-calendar run.

---

## The Core Insight

Attention is the asset. Everything else is execution. A gym that owns the attention of its local market does not need to compete on price or features. People join gyms they feel connected to — and connection is built through content that shows up consistently, speaks in a human voice, and earns trust before it ever asks for money.

The fatal mistake: treating content as advertising. Advertising interrupts. Content earns. The gym that runs 3 ads a week is a gym trying to sell. The gym that posts 3 pieces of real, human content every day is a gym people want to be part of before they ever walk in the door.

The second fatal mistake: waiting for perfect. The gym that posts 300 pieces of imperfect content in a year will outperform the gym that posts 30 perfect pieces. This is not theory — it is algorithm mathematics. Volume of attempts plus speed of learning compounds over time.

## Document Don't Create — What It Means in Practice for a Gym

"Document don't create" means: stop trying to produce content. Start capturing what already exists.

Every day, a gym has more interesting material than any content agency could invent:
- A member who just hit a personal milestone
- A coach explaining something in a way that makes a concept click
- A 6am class energy that no stock photo can replicate
- The conversation at the desk when someone finally decides to join
- The moment a 55-year-old woman does her first unassisted pushup
- A member's face when they realize they've come 50 times

None of this requires a script, a production budget, or a content calendar. It requires a phone and the habit of seeing content moments as they happen.

**The document mindset in practice:**

Instead of: "We need to create a video about why our gym is different."
Document: "We need to film the 6am coach's pre-class talk — the one where he explains why consistency beats intensity every time. That's a real moment with real energy."

Instead of: "We need to write a testimonial post."
Document: "Ask Sarah if she'll do a 60-second voice note about what the gym has changed for her — no script, just talking. Post it verbatim."

Instead of: "We should make content about our accountability program."
Document: "Show the actual text thread between a coach and a member at day 10 of the new member program. Blur the last name. That's the accountability program more powerfully than any explainer post."

**The shift in operational posture for the gym team:**
- Every staff member should know: "If something happens that's worth sharing, pull out your phone."
- No staging, no rehearsing, no waiting for the best light
- The rawness is the point — it signals authenticity that polished production cannot fake
- Good enough and real beats perfect and staged

**What counts as documentable in a gym environment:**
- Member milestones (first pullup, first 5K, 100th session, goal achieved)
- Coach moments (a coaching cue that works, a technique explanation, a team huddle)
- Before-the-class energy (music, warmup, the room before people start)
- Behind-the-scenes (equipment setup, early morning opening, cleaning the gym)
- Real conversations (with permission): "What made you finally start?" / "What's been the hardest part?"
- Community moments (members who know each other, inside jokes, the regulars)

**What not to document:** Staged transformation photos. Posed "in-gym" model shots. Anyone without explicit permission. Anything that would feel uncomfortable if the person in it saw it.

## What Makes a Cornerstone Piece — and How to Identify One in a Gym Environment

A cornerstone piece is a single long-form piece of content that captures something true, valuable, and emotionally resonant — and can be broken down into 30 or more derivative pieces.

**Characteristics of a cornerstone piece:**
1. It says something specific and real — not "gym motivation" but "why most people quit the gym in week 3 and what actually prevents it"
2. It has enough substance to be broken into multiple angles
3. It is evergreen — relevant in January and in July
4. It is native to your real experience — something your coaches or members have actually lived, not something you read in a fitness article

**How to identify a cornerstone piece in a gym:**

Ask the head coach: "What's the one thing you wish every new member understood before they started that would change their experience completely?" Record the answer. That's a cornerstone.

Ask your most retained member: "What's different about this gym compared to everywhere else you've tried?" Record the answer. That's a cornerstone.

Pull the most common objection from the sales desk: "What do people say when they hesitate to join?" The answer to that objection, told through a real story, is a cornerstone.

**Examples of cornerstone pieces for a gym:**

1. A 10-minute coach monologue: "What 10 years of helping people start their fitness journey taught me about why most people quit — and the one thing that predicts who stays"
2. A member sit-down interview: "Sarah joined at 49 after quitting three other gyms. Here's what she did differently this time." (Full interview, 8–12 minutes)
3. A documentary-style short: "What actually happens in the first 30 days at [gym name] — filmed in real time with four new members" (15–20 minutes)
4. A written piece: "The complete guide to starting at a gym for the first time as an adult — written by the coaches who've seen it all"
5. An annotated workout video: "We filmed a real first-day member workout and our coach narrated everything — what we're thinking, why we program it this way, what to expect"

## The 30 Pieces From 1 — Complete Breakdown

Take Cornerstone Example 2: A 12-minute member interview with Sarah (joined at 49, had quit three gyms before, stayed for 14 months and counting).

**Long-form originals (3 pieces):**
1. Full interview video — YouTube / Facebook Watch (12 min)
2. Full written transcript as a blog post — website SEO
3. Audio version — podcast episode or Facebook audio post

**Medium-form cuts (6 pieces):**
4. 60-second highlight reel — "The moment Sarah decided to try again" — Instagram Reels / Facebook Reel
5. 90-second clip — "What Sarah said when I asked her what's different this time" — YouTube Short
6. 60-second clip — "Sarah on the fear of being judged at the gym" — Instagram Reel
7. 45-second clip — "Three gyms. Three quits. What finally changed." — TikTok / Instagram Reel
8. 60-second clip — "14 months in. What Sarah's life looks like now." — Facebook video
9. 90-second clip — "Sarah's first week — what actually happened" — YouTube Short

**Quote graphics (5 pieces):**
10. Pull quote card: "I kept waiting to be ready. But I was never going to be ready until I just went." — Sarah (styled graphic, Instagram / Facebook static post)
11. Pull quote card: "The thing that kept me coming back wasn't the workout. It was that they noticed when I wasn't there." — Sarah
12. Pull quote card: "I've lost 18 pounds. But that's not the thing I'm proud of. I'm proud that I stopped quitting."
13. Pull quote card: "I told myself I was too old. I was 49. I was wrong."
14. Pull quote card: "My daughter texted me last week and said 'Mom, you're different.' That was the moment I cried."

**Platform-native text posts (5 pieces):**
15. Facebook long-form post: Tell Sarah's story in full written form, in first or second person, ending with a soft CTA — "If any part of Sarah's story sounds like yours, we should talk."
16. Instagram caption story: Same story but compressed to 3 short paragraphs — raw, direct, no fluff
17. Google Business Profile post: "Member spotlight — Sarah, member since 2023" — short, warm, with photo
18. Community group post (local Facebook group): Share something relevant — "We've had three members in their late 40s join this year who told us the same thing. That they thought the gym was 'not for them anymore.' This is Sarah's story."
19. Email newsletter section: Sarah's story as the lead story in the monthly member newsletter — 3 paragraphs, links to the full video

**Interactive/engagement pieces (4 pieces):**
20. Poll (Instagram Stories / Facebook): "Has fear of judgment ever stopped you from going to a gym? Yes / No / Sometimes"
21. Question prompt (Instagram Stories): "What's the biggest thing that stopped you from joining a gym before? Reply below."
22. "React if this was you" Facebook post — short paragraph about waiting to feel ready before starting
23. Caption contest or fill-in: "Finish this sentence: I finally started going to the gym when ___"

**Behind-the-scenes / process pieces (4 pieces):**
24. Instagram Story series: "Behind the scenes of filming Sarah's interview" — 5-frame story showing the process
25. "What Sarah told us that didn't make the video" — short follow-up post with one additional detail
26. Coach response video: Coach watches the clip of Sarah talking about why she almost quit and responds — "Here's what our coaches do differently in the first 30 days specifically because of people like Sarah"
27. Staff share: coach or front desk shares the video with a personal 2-sentence note about what it means to them

**SEO / evergreen pieces (3 pieces):**
28. Blog post: "Why adults in their 40s and 50s quit gyms — and what one member's story reveals about the real reason"
29. FAQ article: "Is it too late to start working out at 49? Here's what the research says — and what our members prove"
30. Google Business Profile Q&A response: Answer the question "Is your gym good for beginners or people who haven't worked out in years?" — reference real member results

**Total: 30 pieces from one 12-minute interview.**

## Platform-Native Content Guidelines

What works on one platform often fails on another. AHRI adapts content to the platform, not the platform to the content.

### Facebook

**Who is here:** Adults 30–65. The lifestyle member avatar's primary platform. Parents, professionals, people who grew up with Facebook and stayed. Decision-makers with disposable income.

**What works:**
- Long-form storytelling posts (300–800 words) — Facebook rewards reading time
- Real member stories told with emotional arc
- Video content 1–3 minutes (not short-form — Facebook users watch longer)
- Community engagement prompts — "Have you ever..." questions
- Live video — Facebook notifies followers, creates real-time engagement

**Tone:** Warm, conversational, slightly more formal than Instagram. Like a Facebook post from a person you trust, not a brand you follow.

**Posting frequency:** 1 post per day minimum. 1 story per day. Go Live 1–2x per week.

**What fails on Facebook:**
- Overly polished production — triggers "this is an ad" response
- Hashtag overload — 1–2 max, sometimes zero
- Short disconnected posts — the algorithm favors content that holds attention
- Posting only offers and promotions — page gets deprioritized

**CTA style:** Soft and direct. "If this sounds like something you've been looking for, send us a message." Or a phone number. Or "Comment 'info' below and we'll reach out." Never an aggressive "SIGN UP NOW" button energy.

---

### Instagram

**Who is here:** Adults 25–45. More visual, faster consumption, higher aspiration orientation. The lifestyle member's secondary platform. They scroll Instagram more impulsively than Facebook — the hook must land in the first half-second.

**What works:**
- Reels (15–60 seconds) — algorithmic distribution to non-followers. This is discovery content.
- Before/after comparisons — NOT body transformation (compliance risk). Before/after of energy, habits, confidence.
- Real people, real moments — UGC (user-generated content) outperforms produced content
- Carousels (5–10 slide posts) — saved more often, shown to more people, used for educational content
- Stories — daily, multiple. More casual. Polls, questions, behind the scenes.

**Tone:** Direct, visual-first, conversational. Short captions. Punchy hooks. The visual does 70% of the work; the caption does 30%.

**Posting frequency:** 1 Reel per day (or minimum 5 per week). 3–5 Stories per day. 2–3 feed posts per week.

**What fails on Instagram:**
- Text-heavy graphics that look like slides
- Stock photography
- Formal brand language — Instagram audiences can detect inauthenticity immediately
- Posting once a week — the algorithm treats infrequent posters as irrelevant

**CTA style:** "Link in bio" (Instagram's constraint). Or "DM us '[keyword]' and we'll send you the details." Or "Comment below." Keep it native to the platform.

---

### Google Business Profile

**Who is here:** Level 4–5 awareness prospects (see schwartz-awareness.md). People who have already decided they want a gym and are evaluating options. Highest intent traffic of any platform. They are one review or one compelling post away from booking a visit.

**What works:**
- Member testimonials and reviews (actively ask for these — they are more valuable than any ad)
- Weekly posts that highlight current offers or programs
- Q&A responses that address every common objection
- Photo updates showing real gym life (not staged)
- Event posts for community events, challenges, or cohort starts

**Tone:** Professional but human. This is an evaluation platform — prospects are deciding. Give them concrete reasons to choose you over the gym down the street.

**Posting frequency:** 1 post per week minimum. Respond to every review within 24 hours (positive and negative). Add new photos weekly.

**What fails on GBP:**
- Ignoring it entirely — 40%+ of local searches use GBP to make decisions
- Not responding to negative reviews — prospects read owner responses more than the reviews themselves
- Generic posts copied from other platforms

## The Jab Jab Jab Right Hook Model — Applied to Fitness

**Jab:** A piece of content that gives value without asking for anything in return. Earns goodwill, trust, attention. No offer, no CTA, no pitch. Just value.

**Right hook:** A direct ask. A specific offer with a specific CTA. The moment you convert attention into action.

**The ratio:** 3:1 minimum. For a cold audience being built: 5:1 or higher. You cannot right-hook a cold audience. You must earn the right to ask.

**What counts as a jab for a gym:**

| Content Type | Why It's a Jab |
|---|---|
| "3 exercises for better posture if you sit at a desk all day" | Useful to the avatar, no pitch, gives value freely |
| "Here's why your energy crashes at 3pm (and what one 20-minute habit changes it)" | Education, no gym required to use the advice |
| Member story with no offer at the end | Social proof as entertainment, no ask |
| Behind-the-scenes coach moment | Human connection, no conversion ask |
| Motivational content that resonates with the avatar's life | Emotional connection without pitching |
| "Ask us anything about starting a gym" Q&A | Service orientation, expertise without barrier |

**What counts as a right hook for a gym:**

| Content Type | Why It's a Right Hook |
|---|---|
| "We have 4 spots in the January cohort — here's how to claim one" | Specific offer, specific action required |
| "DM us 'start' and we'll get you set up this week" | Direct CTA, clear action |
| Paid ad with lead form | Conversion-first content |
| "Call us today — this offer ends Friday" | Urgency + CTA |
| "Book a free intro visit — link in bio" | Asks for contact information in exchange |

**The mistake local gyms make:** Right-hooking too early or too often. A page that posts "Join now — $1 trial!" every week without earned trust gets unfollowed, muted, or ignored. The audience has to feel known before they will act.

**The cadence in practice:**
- Week 1–4 (new page): All jabs. Zero right hooks. Build the audience.
- Month 2 onward: 5:1 ratio for cold audiences / 3:1 ratio for warm
- Active campaign weeks (January, spring): Allow 2:1 briefly — but return to 3:1 after

## Volume Beats Perfection — The Specific Reason for Local Gyms

**The math argument:**
A gym that posts once a week creates 52 contact points per year with its audience.
A gym that posts daily creates 365 contact points.
The daily-posting gym has 7x more opportunities for someone to see a piece of content that lands at the exact moment they are ready to take action.

The "ready" moment is not predictable. A prospect might have been passively following the gym for 3 months. The moment they get out of breath chasing their kid, or catch themselves in a mirror, or hear a doctor mention "exercise more" — that is when they will act. The gym that was there 365 times is infinitely more likely to be top-of-mind at that moment than the gym that was there 52 times.

**The quality counterargument and why it fails locally:**
High-quality production serves brand building at national scale. At local scale (within a 5-mile radius of a single gym), production quality is irrelevant compared to presence, consistency, and human authenticity. A member watching a shaky phone video of their coach explaining something useful will engage more than a polished commercial.

**The learning argument:**
Posting daily produces 30 pieces of content in a month. After month 1, you have data: which formats got engagement, which hooks landed, which topics generated comments. After month 3, you have real signal. After 6 months, you know exactly what your local audience responds to. The gym posting once a week knows almost nothing after 6 months. The daily poster has made 180 decisions and learned from each one.

**The standard:** Post something every day. It does not have to be long. It does not have to be produced. It has to be real, relevant to the avatar, and consistent in showing up.

## How to Document Real Gym Life Without Disrupting Operations

**The 3-minute rule:** If capturing a content moment takes more than 3 minutes, it is disrupting operations. Most content moments take under 60 seconds to capture.

**Content capture protocol for gym staff:**
- One staff member is designated "content eye" per shift. Rotates weekly.
- Content eye has permission to pull out phone at any point to capture something real.
- All captures go into a shared folder (no curation required — just capture).
- AHRI/operator reviews folder weekly and selects what to post.

**Moments to watch for:**
- A member doing something for the first time (first rep, first class, first consultation)
- A coach explaining something with particular clarity or energy
- The 6am energy before class — the shared experience of choosing to be there
- A milestone moment (the chart on the wall when someone hits 100 sessions)
- A member laughing, celebrating, or connecting with another member
- A quiet moment — the gym at 5:30am before anyone else arrives

**What requires explicit permission:**
- Any video or photo of a member's face
- Any quote or story attributed to a specific person
- Any health or body-related content featuring a member

**Permission workflow:**
- Have member sign a media release (included in membership paperwork, but re-confirm per use for sensitive content)
- Or simply ask: "Hey, can I post this? It's really good." Most members who are proud of something say yes immediately.
- If unsure: blur faces, use first names only, or ask

**The gym's content creation cost when done right:** Near zero. A phone, a shared folder, and a weekly review session of 30 minutes. The content itself is generated by the gym's daily operation — you are just capturing what already exists.
