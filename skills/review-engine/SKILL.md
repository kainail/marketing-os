# Review Engine — Skill Instructions

You are AHRI running the review-engine skill. Your job is to generate a complete Google review acquisition and management system: the right ask at each high-emotion moment, the response templates that convert skeptics, and the velocity strategy that builds a review profile that wins local search.

**The business case before generating anything:**

A gym with 200 reviews at 4.9 stars is a different business than a gym with 20 reviews at 4.2 stars — even if they are the same gym. The 200-review gym:
- Ranks higher in the Google Maps local pack for "gym near me" and "personal trainer [city]"
- Gets clicked 3-4x more than the 4.2-star competitor
- Converts prospect visits at a meaningfully higher rate because trust is pre-established before the conversation starts
- Makes every paid ad cheaper because the social proof load is already carried by organic review trust
- Has an asset that compounds — each new review strengthens the ones before it

The review profile is not a vanity metric. It is an acquisition asset that works 24 hours a day without a budget. Most gyms ignore it completely. A gym that works this system is building a permanent competitive moat that money cannot replicate.

---

## The timing rule — non-negotiable

**Never ask for a review:**
- In the first 2 weeks (too little experience to write a meaningful review)
- After a difficult or frustrating session (wrong emotional state — a forced positive review reads as hollow)
- Via a mass email blast (feels automated — the prospect can tell and the reviews reflect that)
- With a financial incentive of any kind (violates Google's Terms of Service and can result in review removal or penalties)

**Always ask at peak positive emotional experience.** The member who writes a review at peak satisfaction writes a detailed, specific, genuinely persuasive review. The member who writes a review because they got an email from a CRM writes "Great gym! Friendly staff. 5 stars."

The second type of review is not worthless. But the first type converts skeptical prospects. That is the difference AHRI is optimizing for.

- Contains the direct Google review link (one tap — no searching)

**Email messages (anniversary moments only):**

- Under 75 words for positive reviews. Under 100 words for negative reviews. Under 50 words for one-liners and spam.

## The 5 Review Request Moments

### MOMENT 1 — After the Orientation Session

**Why this moment:**
The orientation session is the highest positive emotional spike in the first 30 days for most new members. The member just had a 1-on-1 with a coach, saw the gym, understood the program, and — most importantly — found out it is much less intimidating than they feared. That fear was their primary barrier for potentially months or years. When it is removed in 30 minutes, the emotional relief and gratitude is significant.

This is also the highest-volume trigger: every new member goes through orientation. Even if only 20% write a review, over 12 months at 20 new members per month, that is 48 reviews from this trigger alone.

**Signal:** Orientation session just ended. Member is still in the building. Coach or staff member walks them to the door.

---

**In-person script:**
Structure: (1) Reflect the specific thing that just happened — what changed for them in this session. (2) The bridge to the ask. (3) The phone handoff — smooth, natural.

Write the script. Under 3 sentences. It sounds like the coach wrapping up a real conversation.

**Follow-up if YES (they say yes to the link):**

**Follow-up if NOT RIGHT NOW (they're in a rush, they say "sure, send it later"):**
Write the exact words. This triggers the SMS (below) sent within the hour.

### MOMENT 3 — After a Positive Check-in Response

**Why this moment:**
This trigger is the most organic. A member replies to a routine check-in text with something positive — "feeling great this week," "slept better than I have in months," "finally kept up with my son." The emotional energy is already present. AHRI (or the staff) simply redirects it.

**Signal:** The member responds positively to any GHL automated check-in message. The GHL workflow flags the response for immediate human follow-up.

**This trigger requires a human response — not an automated one.** The member opened up in a personal channel. Responding with an automated review request destroys the moment. The correct response is a genuine, personal reply — and then, at the end of that reply, the ask.

### MOMENT 5 — Anniversary Milestones

**Why this moment:**
Anniversary members have enough experience to write a meaningful, specific review. ## Review Response Templates

AHRI generates 8 response templates. Kai uses these to respond to all incoming reviews within 24 hours. Responses should never be copy-pasted verbatim — Kai personalizes with specific details from the actual review — but these templates provide the structure and tone that is consistent across all responses.

**The most important rule about review responses:** Prospects read negative review responses more carefully than positive reviews. A gym that handles a critical review with grace and accountability attracts the prospect who is evaluating risk. A gym that responds defensively or not at all signals that the experience described might be typical.

---

### Template 1 — 5-Star Enthusiastic (Detailed Review)
**When to use:** Reviewer wrote multiple sentences about a specific experience, a specific staff member, or a specific outcome.
**Approach:** Warm, specific to what they wrote, never generic, references something from their review, invites continued engagement.
**Under 75 words.**

Generate 3 variations — same structure, different language, so responses to similar reviews don't all look identical.

---

### Template 2 — 5-Star Brief (One or Two Words from Reviewer)
**When to use:** Reviewer gave 5 stars and wrote "Great gym!" or "Love it!" — minimal text.
**Approach:** Warmer than the review deserved. Makes them feel seen for even a brief review. Names what the gym is proud of without being braggy.
**Under 50 words.**

Generate 1 variation with guidance on personalizing it.

---

### Template 3 — 4-Star (Good Experience, Implied Gap)
**When to use:** Reviewer gave 4 stars — the experience was good but something was not quite right. The review may or may not name the specific issue.
**Approach:**
1. Acknowledge the rating graciously — not "oh no, why only 4 stars"
2. Thank them specifically for what they praised
3. Address the implied gap: "I'd love to understand what would have made it a 5-star experience for you."
4. Move the conversation offline: "Can I reach out directly?"
**Under 75 words.**

---

### Template 4 — 3-Star or Below (Negative Experience)
**When to use:** Reviewer gave 1, 2, or 3 stars. They had a genuinely bad experience.
**Approach — the most important template to get right:**
1. Never defensive. Never begin with "We're sorry you feel that way" (invalidating). Never begin by correcting factual claims in public.
2. Begin with full acknowledgment of their experience: "Thank you for taking the time to share this."
3. Take ownership without admitting specific fault that isn't established: "We clearly didn't deliver the experience you deserved."
4. Move offline immediately: "I'd really like to understand what happened and make it right. Can I reach out to you directly?"
5. Close warmly — not robotically.
**Under 100 words.**

**The strategic reasoning Kai should understand:** A prospect who reads a 1-star review and then reads a genuinely humble, accountable owner response learns more about this gym's character than from any 5-star review. The response converts the negative review into a trust signal. Respond to every negative review within 24 hours.

---

### Template 5 — Fake or Spam Review
**When to use:** The review is clearly from someone who was never a member — wrong location, wrong business name, generic attack with no specific detail, pattern that matches coordinated spam.
**Approach:** Professional, calm, factual. Do not accuse. Flag the anomaly.
**Under 50 words.**

**Note:** After posting this response, flag the review to Google for removal through Google Business Profile manager. Document the response and the flagging date. If multiple fake reviews appear within a short window, escalate to Kai immediately — this may indicate a competitor action or reputation attack.

## Monthly Review Velocity Strategy

AHRI generates a monthly review acquisition plan calibrated to the gym's current review count and local competitive context.

**Required inputs (read from brain state or flag to Kai if missing):**
- Current Google review count: [KAI — enter current count]
- Current Google star rating: [KAI — enter current rating]
- Nearest competitor review count and rating: [KAI — enter if known. If unknown: flag as intelligence gap and note it in output]
- Target: local pack top 3 position

**AHRI calculates:**
1. How many new reviews per month at current velocity (if known)
2. What count and rating would place this gym in the local pack top 3 for primary search terms
3. How many months at target velocity to reach that position
4. Which of the 5 trigger moments will generate the highest volume this month (based on current member count at each trigger point)

**The velocity warning:**
Google's algorithm flags sudden spikes in review count as suspicious — particularly if they arrive within a short time window. The correct acquisition pace is steady and organic. A gym that goes from 15 reviews to 80 reviews in 30 days will likely see reviews suppressed or the profile flagged. The target pace: 3-8 new reviews per month, consistently, over 12-18 months, builds an unassailable review profile that an algorithm-gaming competitor cannot replicate.

## A/B VARIANT SPLIT

**Variant A — Orientation-focused:**
The primary review acquisition strategy prioritizes Moment 1 (orientation session). The in-person script is maximally refined. Every new member goes through orientation — this is the highest-volume, most consistent trigger.

All 5 scripts and SMS messages are generated, but Variant A's output section leads with Moment 1 and includes a note: "For a gym in growth phase with 20+ new members per month, focusing on Moment 1 alone can generate 4-8 new reviews per month at consistent quality."

**Variant B — Milestone-focused:**
The primary review acquisition strategy prioritizes Moment 2 (30-day completion) and Moment 5 (anniversaries). These produce reviews that are more specific, more detailed, and more persuasive to the prospect who is evaluating the gym — because they come from members with real sustained experience.

Variant B's output section leads with Moment 2 and the anniversary tier emails, noting: "For a gym with an established member base and a strong retention rate, milestone-moment reviews are the highest-quality reviews in your profile. They are also the reviews that convert your most hesitant prospects — because they come from people whose history of quitting matches the prospect's own."

AHRI's recommendation between variants: If the gym has fewer than 50 current Google reviews, Variant A builds volume faster and should run first. If the gym has 50+ reviews, Variant B adds the quality layer that moves the average rating upward and improves the strategic value of the review profile.

## Quality bar

A Google review written by a member who was asked using one of these scripts should be specific enough that a prospect reads it and thinks: "That person is describing exactly what I'm afraid of — and it turned out fine." The ask creates the conditions for that review. Vague asks produce vague reviews. Moment-specific asks, in the right voice, at the right emotional peak, produce the reviews that build the local pack ranking that no competitor can buy.
