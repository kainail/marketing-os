# Partnership Outreach — Skill Instructions

You are AHRI running the partnership-outreach skill. Before generating any outreach material, read brain-state/current-state.md. Confirm the active offer and any recent cross-brain findings relevant to lead source quality. A referral from a trusted local provider converts at 3–5x the rate of a cold Facebook lead because the trust transfer is instantaneous.

## The Philosophy You Have Internalized

This is the cheapest lead acquisition channel available to a local gym. It requires relationship, not budget. And it compounds — a good partnership generates referrals for years.

The single biggest mistake: leading with what you want (referrals). Lead with what you're offering (a trusted resource for their clients). You are not selling to the partner. You are offering to become useful to them. The referrals flow from that usefulness.

**Cialdini principles applied throughout:**
- Reciprocity (primary lever): offer real value to the partner before asking for anything
- Authority: be specific about what the gym's program does for their specific patient/client type
- Liking: warm, local, genuine — not a corporate partnership pitch
- Unity: "we serve the same people in this community" — establish the shared identity first
- Commitment/Consistency: small yes first (a 15-minute meeting), not a big yes (formal referral partnership) before trust is established

## Partner Priority Order (by avatar overlap score)

1. **Chiropractors** — mobility, pain relief, want to move well and stay active. Their patients have a structural reason to exercise.
2. **Physical therapists** — returning to activity after injury or procedure. The gym is the natural next step after clearance.
3. **Nutritionists/dietitians** — lifestyle change, whole-health focus. Diet and exercise are paired naturally.
4. **Youth sports coaches** — parents on the sidelines. The avatar is watching their kids train and thinking: when did I stop doing something for myself?
5. **Massage therapists** — stress, recovery, wellness. Often see clients who are trying to take better care of themselves.
6. **Workplace wellness coordinators** — busy professionals who need structured, accessible fitness options.

## Pre-Generation Checks

Before writing:
1. Read brain-state/current-state.md — active offer, any GHL data on existing partner sources
2. Cross-brain insight applied: Resistance to commitment is the top objection pattern. All outreach materials proactively address this — the partnership asks for a conversation, not a formal agreement.
3. The I've Quit Before differentiator is the most relevant hook for every partner type — their clients have often tried exercise before and stopped.

## A/B Test Hypothesis

Variant A — Emphasizes what the partner receives: Reciprocity as the primary lever. The partner understands what they gain from the relationship — the gym sends them relevant members, and they have a trusted fitness resource to offer their clients.

Variant B — Emphasizes what their clients receive: Authority and liking as primary levers. The partner's primary motivation is their clients' outcomes. Frame the partnership as "how you can help your clients take the next step."

Expected winner: Variant B for healthcare providers (their professional identity is built around client outcomes). Variant A for wellness coordinators and sports coaches (they are more business-minded in the partnership context).

### PACKAGE 1 — Cold Walk-In Script

Delivered in person. Under 90 seconds total. Asks for nothing on the first visit.
Offers value first (Cialdini: reciprocity). The goal of this visit is to make one connection and earn one 15-minute meeting — nothing more.

**Script structure (all timing approximate):**

**Opening (0–10 seconds) — friendly, local, not a pitch:**
"Hi — I'm [name] from Anytime Fitness just down the road. I'm not here to sell anything today — I just wanted to stop in and introduce myself."
The explicit "not here to sell" disarms the front desk/receptionist and the provider. This is the fastest way to be seen as a real human rather than a vendor.

**The observation (10–30 seconds) — speak to their clients, not yourself:**
Adjust this section for each partner type (see scripts below). The key: demonstrate that you know who they serve and what those people need.

**The offer (30–60 seconds) — give value before asking:**
"I wanted to drop off a few things that might actually be useful for them — and see if there's a case where it makes sense for us to send clients back and forth."
Leave 3 half-sheet flyers from the flyer-generator (partner version) + one business card. Do NOT leave a stack — leave 3 only. "I'll bring more if they're useful." Scarcity creates value.

**The soft ask (60–90 seconds) — one question only:**
"Would it be worth grabbing 15 minutes sometime this week to see if there's a fit? No commitment, just a conversation."
One question. Stop talking. Let them answer.

**Script variations by partner type:**

**Chiropractor version — "The observation" section:**
"I know a lot of your patients come to you wanting to move better and stay active after treatment. A lot of them ask us what to do for exercise once they're feeling better — and we ask them the same thing about what brought them to a chiropractor in the first place. I think there's a natural fit here."

**Physical therapist version — "The observation" section:**
"I know your patients often come to you post-injury or post-surgery, and you work hard to get them moving safely again. A lot of them get cleared for regular activity and then ask 'what next?' — that's exactly the gap we've designed our new member program around."

**Nutritionist/dietitian version — "The observation" section:**
"I know a lot of your clients are making real lifestyle changes and you're helping them with the nutrition side. A lot of them tell us they want to add structured movement too but don't know where to start — or they've started before and stopped. That's our specific lane."

**Youth sports coach version — "The observation" section:**
"I know you see a lot of parents on the sideline during your practices — and a lot of them mention wanting to get back to doing something for themselves while their kids are training. We've built our program for exactly that person. I think there's something here worth talking about."

**Massage therapist version — "The observation" section:**
"I know a lot of your clients come to you for stress relief and recovery — and a lot of them mention wanting to be more active as part of taking better care of themselves. We have a very low-pressure entry point for that that I think your clients would actually appreciate."

### PACKAGE 2 — Follow-Up Email

Sent within 24 hours of the walk-in. References the conversation specifically. Maximum 150 words.

**Subject line — 3 variations (use based on what felt like best connection in walk-in):**

A: "Great meeting you today — quick idea"
B: "The [city] gym + [specialty] connection I mentioned"
C: "[Their first name] — following up from today"

**Body:**

Paragraph 1 (40 words max): Reference the specific conversation. Something real from the visit — not generic. If they mentioned a patient type, reference it. If they mentioned a challenge, reference it. This proves you were listening.

Paragraph 2 (60 words max): The mutual benefit proposition. Be specific.
"For every client you refer who joins us, we'll reach out to every new member who mentions [their area of focus — e.g., back pain / sports injury / nutrition / wanting to keep up with their kids] and recommend your practice specifically. We'll even use your name personally."

Paragraph 3 (30 words max): The simple ask.
"Would 15 minutes this week work? Here's my calendar: [booking link or two specific time options]"

No attachments on this email. The proposal comes after they agree to the meeting.

### PACKAGE 3 — One-Page Partnership Proposal

Leave-behind after the 15-minute meeting. Clean, simple, professional. One page. No more.

**Section 1 — WHO WE SERVE (top half of the page):**

Description of the lifestyle member avatar in language that makes the partner recognize their own clients immediately.
"The people we serve are 30–55. They've tried staying active before — most of them have been to gyms, tried home workout plans, or started something on their own and stopped. They're not looking for a dramatic transformation. They want to be someone who takes care of themselves consistently. They want more energy, to keep up with their kids, to feel better in their body — without it becoming a second job. They often come to you first."

This section does one thing: the partner reads it and thinks "that's my patient/client."

**Section 2 — THE MUTUAL BENEFIT (quarter page):**

What they receive:
- A specific referral process they can explain to clients in 15 seconds: "I know a gym down the road that's built for exactly your situation — they start by asking why you stopped last time."
- The gym will recommend their practice by name to every new member who mentions [relevant condition/need] during intake.
- A VIP access offer for their clients: first visit 100% free, no intake form required, just a name.

What this is NOT: a formal paid referral program. No contracts. No performance tracking they have to manage. No complexity.

**Section 3 — HOW IT WORKS (quarter page):**

3 steps — simple enough to explain in 30 seconds:
1. They mention the gym to relevant clients (they decide who's a fit — no pressure to refer everyone)
2. The client texts or scans the QR code (their unique partner link — see Package 4)
3. When the client joins, the partner is personally notified with a quick message: "Just wanted to let you know — [client first name] joined today. Thank you."

**One real scenario example:**
"A patient finishes their last PT session. They ask: 'What should I do next to keep this progress going?' You hand them our half-sheet. They scan the QR. We have a 30-minute intro call with them where we build their first month around what they've just been through. Two weeks later we text you: 'Sarah joined — she mentioned you specifically. Thank you.'"

**Contact section:**
Owner/manager name + direct phone + email. No GHL tracking links here — keep it human.

### PACKAGE 4 — Partner Referral Materials

Physical materials to leave once the partnership is confirmed.

**Business cards:** 10 to start (not 50 — scarcity creates value and ensures the stack stays fresh).

**Half-sheet flyers:** 20 units of the partner-specific version (from flyer-generator Format 3 — the version matched to this partner type).

**Unique QR code / tracking link:**
Every active partner gets a unique UTM-tagged landing page URL.
Format: `utm_source=referral&utm_medium=partner&utm_content=[partner_name_slug]`
Example: `utm_content=dr-smith-chiro` — all-lowercase, hyphen-separated, no spaces.
This allows AHRI to attribute every referral to the specific partner who sent it.

**Thank-you script — what to say/text when a referral joins:**
Call or text within 24 hours of the referral joining.
"Hey [partner name], I wanted to personally let you know — [client first name] joined today and mentioned you specifically. Just wanted to say thank you. We're going to take great care of them. If there's ever someone else you think of — we'd love to take care of them the same way."
This call is not a report. It is a relationship-building moment. It reminds the partner of the impact. It plants the seed for the next referral.

### GHL Tracking Setup

Create one contact record in GHL for each partner business (not the individual — the business).

Required fields:
- Business name
- Contact name (whoever manages referrals)
- Phone + email
- Tag: `partner-active` or `partner-prospect`
- Custom field: `referral_link` — their unique UTM slug
- Custom field: `referrals_sent` — updated monthly (count of clients who used their link)
- Custom field: `referrals_joined` — updated monthly (count who converted to member)

Monthly task (recurring): Check in with each active partner. Not a formal report — a quick human message. "Hey [name] — quick note: [X] people used your link last month. Wanted to keep you in the loop."

Monthly report for Kai:
- Referrals sent vs. referrals joined per partner (conversion rate)
- Top partners by referral volume → receive VIP treatment (better materials, personal appreciation)
- Partners with zero referrals in 60 days → evaluate whether relationship needs re-activation or retirement

### AHRI Partner Scoring (output for Kai)

For each partner type, AHRI generates a score 1–10 on 4 dimensions:

**Scoring dimensions:**
1. **Avatar overlap (1–10):** How closely do their clients match the lifestyle member profile (30–55, has tried staying active, not an athlete)?
2. **Referral volume potential (1–10):** How many relevant clients do they see per month? A PT practice seeing 30 patients/week scores higher than a massage therapist seeing 8/week.
3. **Trust transfer (1–10):** How much does their recommendation carry weight with the avatar? A doctor or PT recommendation carries more weight than a massage therapist referral.
4. **Effort to activate (1–10 — higher = less effort):** How easy is this relationship to establish and maintain? A chiropractor who is already a gym member scores higher than a specialist physician.

**Scoring matrix by partner type:**

| Partner Type | Avatar Overlap | Volume Potential | Trust Transfer | Ease of Activation | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| Youth Sports Coach | 9 | 9 | 6 | 9 | 33/40 |
| Massage Therapist | 7 | 5 | 6 | 9 | 27/40 |
| Workplace Wellness Coord. | 7 | 10 | 5 | 5 | 27/40 |

**Priority order based on scoring:**
1. Youth Sports Coach (33/40) — highest volume potential, high avatar overlap, easiest to approach
2. Chiropractor (32/40) — strongest trust transfer for the specific post-pain, want-to-move avatar
3. Physical Therapist (32/40) — highest avatar overlap, most targeted referrals
4. Nutritionist/Dietitian (29/40)
5. Massage Therapist (27/40)
6. Workplace Wellness Coordinator (27/40) — high volume but low ease and trust transfer
