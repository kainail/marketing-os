# Lifestyle Member Avatar — Anytime Fitness

**Avatar Name:** The Lifestyle Member
**File:** lifestyle-member.md
**Status:** Primary avatar — default for all campaigns until data warrants a change

---

## Who This Person Is

The Lifestyle Member is 30–55 years old. They are not trying to become an athlete. They are trying to become someone who takes care of themselves consistently. They have tried gyms before — possibly multiple times — and stopped going. They know what working out feels like. They know how good they feel when they do it. The problem is they keep stopping.

They are not a beginner in the sense of not knowing what exercise is. They are a beginner in the sense of not yet having built an identity around going consistently.

This person is the core of a small gym's business. They have the income to sustain a membership. They have the motivation to start. What they lack is the structure and accountability that makes starting stick.

---

## Demographics

**Age:** 32–55 (core: 38–48)
**Gender:** Skews female slightly (55/45 F/M) but strong male segment exists — do not default to female-only copy
**Income:** Middle-to-upper-middle. $65,000–$150,000 household income. Gym membership is not a financial stretch — it is a priority question.
**Life stage:** Established adults. Kids may be older (school-age or teens). Career is settled. They have bandwidth to invest in themselves if they decide it's worth it.
**Location:** Suburban and small-city. Not urban gym-dense markets where 20 gyms compete on every block.
**Education:** College-educated. Can read nuance. Does not respond to hype.

---

## Psychographics

**Their core identity belief:** "I am someone who should be working out, but I'm not right now."

This gap — between who they know they should be and who they are — is the source of low-level guilt they carry almost daily. It surfaces when they get winded walking up stairs. When they see a photo of themselves they don't recognize. When they have less energy than they think they should. When they can't keep up with their kids.

**What they tell themselves about why they haven't started:**
- "I've been too busy lately — things will calm down."
- "I need to lose a little weight first so I'm not embarrassed at the gym." (The gym is the solution, but this person thinks it's a prerequisite)
- "I've tried this before and it didn't stick. What's different this time?"
- "I don't want to commit to something I'm going to quit in three weeks."
- "I just need to find the right routine."

**What they actually fear:**
1. Failing again — specifically, the shame of paying for something and not using it
2. Being embarrassed at the gym — not knowing what to do, feeling judged by fit people
3. Spending money on something that doesn't produce a visible result
4. Disrupting their already-stretched routine for something that doesn't last

**What they secretly want:**
- To feel like someone who takes care of themselves without it being a big dramatic effort
- To have more energy — not for performance, for daily life
- To feel at home in their body again
- To stop carrying the low-level guilt of not working out
- A habit that becomes automatic — not something they have to motivate themselves into every single time

---

## The Dream Outcome (in their words)

> "I just want to be someone who goes. Not someone who's trying to go. Someone who goes."

The aspiration is identity, not performance. They don't want a six-pack. They want to stop being the person who cancels on themselves.

Secondary outcomes they'll say out loud:
- "More energy"
- "Better sleep"
- "Feel better in my clothes"
- "Keep up with my kids"
- "Get ahead of the health stuff before it becomes a problem"

---

## The Deepest Fear (the one they won't say out loud)

> "What if I try this and I fail again? What does that mean about me?"

Every previous gym attempt that ended in cancellation left a small scar. The fear isn't just about wasting money. It's about what it would mean to be someone who tries and gives up again. The emotional stakes of starting are therefore very high. The safer emotional choice is to not start — because you can't fail at something you never tried.

This is why the offer must remove failure risk explicitly. The guarantee and the accountability structure are not sales tactics — they are the answer to this specific fear.

---

## Ranked Objections (1 = most important to address)

1. **"I've quit before and I don't want to fail again."** — This is the primary objection for every conversion asset targeting this avatar. The answer is the accountability structure and the guarantee.

2. **"I'm too busy / I don't have time."** — The 24/7 access and the flexibility of Anytime Fitness is the direct answer. No class schedules to hit. Go when it works for your life.

3. **"I don't know where to start / I'll be lost."** — The onboarding session and coach relationship answers this. You never walk in and figure it out alone.

4. **"I'm not in good enough shape to go to a gym."** — Social environment and approachability answer this. Real members, not gym models. Coaches who work with beginners.

5. **"It's not the right time."** — The right time is never going to arrive. This is the "doing nothing" alternative — address it honestly, never shame it.

6. **"It's too expensive."** — Price vs. value reframe. The $1 trial is the tactical answer. The LTV of feeling good every day for the next 18 months is the strategic answer.

## Platform Behavior

**Facebook:**
- Primary discovery platform for this avatar (skews 35–55 on Facebook)
- Stops scrolling for real stories from real people — not professional fitness content
- Will watch a 60–90 second video of a real member or coach if the hook lands in the first 5 seconds
- Responds to community proof: sees others like them who have succeeded
- Shares content with friends when it resonates ("this is exactly how I feel")

**Instagram:**
- Supplementary discovery — especially 32–42 age range
- Browses Reels; static posts reach existing followers more than new audiences
- Strong for before/after stories EXCEPT: Meta policy prohibits these in ads. Organic stories are different from paid ads.
- Real gym content (action shots, group energy, face-forward authenticity) outperforms polished production

**Google Search:**
- Highest intent channel — searching "gym near me" or "[city] gym with personal training"
- These searches happen when the decision has already been made to start looking
- Landing page must match search intent exactly — no bait and switch

**Email:**
- Reads email if the subject line is specific and personal-feeling
- Will open 3–4 emails in a nurture sequence before making a decision
- Subject lines that work: curiosity + specificity + relevance to their moment
- Subject lines that don't: generic fitness enthusiasm, hype, obvious automation

## Cialdini Affinities — What Works for This Avatar

**Highest impact:**
- **Unity** — "People like you have done this." Belonging to a group they identify with. This is the most powerful principle for this avatar because the fear of being the outsider is real.
- **Social Proof** — Specific, real stories from members who looked exactly like them 90 days ago. Not general testimonials — specific before-context + specific result.
- **Commitment/Consistency** — The $1 trial creates a micro-commitment. Once they show up once, the identity starts to form. "I'm someone who goes to this gym."

**Supporting principles:**
- **Liking** — Warm, local, genuinely friendly tone. They can tell when copy is sincere vs. manufactured.
- **Authority** — Coaches with real credentials and demonstrated expertise. Not generic "certified trainer" — specific areas of expertise and the member outcomes they've produced.
- **Scarcity** — REAL only. Cohort-based enrollment with genuine spot limits works here. Fake countdown timers do not — this avatar is skeptical and will notice.
- **Reciprocity** — Lead magnets, free guides, content that is genuinely useful before asking for anything.

---

## What AHRI Should Always Know About This Avatar

1. **Identity shift is the real product.** They are not buying a gym membership. They are buying the identity of "someone who goes to the gym." Every asset should help them see that identity as achievable for someone like them.

2. **The accountability structure is the differentiator.** They have had memberships before. What they haven't had is someone who noticed when they stopped. Name this explicitly.

3. **Never shame the past attempts.** The tone around previous quitting is always compassionate. "You've tried. You know what it's like to start and stop. This is what's different." Never "stop making excuses."

4. **Specificity beats aspiration.** "More energy for your morning commute" beats "transform your life." "Kept up with my kids on a 3-mile hike for the first time in 5 years" beats "get fit."

5. **The "quit before" objection must be answered in every conversion asset.** Not just once in the funnel — in every piece of copy that is designed to convert this avatar. It is never resolved permanently; it lives in them until the first week of consistent attendance.
