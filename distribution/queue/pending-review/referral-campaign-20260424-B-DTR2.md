# SECTION 3: INCENTIVE MODEL ANALYSIS AND RECOMMENDATION

---

## Model A: Referrer-Only Reward

**What the referring member receives:** One complimentary month of membership (highest perceived value for a member who already values their membership enough to maintain it).

**What the referred friend receives:** Standard No-Risk Comeback offer — $1/30 days — no enhancement.

**Scoring:**

- **Believability (1–10): 7** — A free month is a real, tangible reward. Members understand it. However, the transactional frame ("I get something if my friend joins") subtly corrupts the social quality of the referral. The referring member may hesitate to ask because they don't want to appear self-interested.
- **Referrer motivation (1–10): 8** — A free month is meaningful. It is also immediately understood — no calculation required.
- **Cost to gym per referred member who converts: ~$54.17** — One free month at standard rate. Acceptable given LTV of ~$975 for a standard member.
- **Likelihood of execution (1–10): 6** — Staff must remember to explain the reward and fulfill it reliably. Fulfillment friction reduces execution.
- **Total score: 31/40**

---

## Model B: Referred-Friend-Only Reward

**What the referring member receives:** Nothing material. The act of doing something good for a friend is the complete reward.

**What the referred friend receives:** An enhanced offer not publicly available. Recommendation: the standard $1/30-day trial plus one complimentary personal training intro session (value: $60–$100) — booked at intake, not offered as a coupon.

**Scoring:**

- **Believability (1–10): 9** — There is no transaction. The referring member cannot be seen as self-interested. The social quality of the referral is fully intact. The referred friend receives something real.
- **Referrer motivation (1–10): 6** — No material reward reduces the explicit incentive. However, for the lifestyle member whose Cialdini affinities are Unity and Commitment/Consistency, the act of helping someone they care about is genuinely motivating — they have built an identity here, and inviting someone in extends that identity.
- **Cost to gym per referred member who converts: ~$70–100** — One comp PT intro session. If that session converts to a PT package, cost becomes negligible against PT LTV.
- **Likelihood of execution (1–10): 8** — The ask is cleaner. Staff don't have to explain a reward structure. The scripts are shorter and more natural.
- **Total score: 33/40**

---

## Model C: Both Rewarded (Smaller Rewards)

**What the referring member receives:** One free personal training session (value: $60–$100) — triggered when the referred friend completes their first 30 days.

**What the referred friend receives:** A modest enhancement — one extra week on the $1 trial (37 days instead of 30).

**Scoring:**

- **Believability (1–10): 6** — The dual reward structure introduces complexity. The referring member must wait for their friend to complete 30 days — a fulfillment delay that reduces perceived value. The referred friend's enhancement is minor and may not feel meaningfully different from the standard offer.
- **Referrer motivation (1–10): 7** — A PT session is appealing, especially for members who haven't tried PT. Opens an upsell path.
- **Cost to gym per referred member who converts: ~$60–130** — PT session plus the extended trial week.
- **Likelihood of execution (1–10): 5** — The most complex model to administer. The 30-day fulfillment delay requires reliable GHL automation. Errors in fulfillment damage the referring member relationship.
- **Total score: 28/40**

---

## AHRI Recommendation: Model B

**For the lifestyle member avatar at this stage, Model B is the correct structure.**

The lifestyle member joined because they wanted to stop being someone who quits. The gym that helped them do that has become part of their identity. When they refer someone, they are not participating in a rewards program — they are acting as a person who has found something real and wants to give it to someone they care about. That motivation is identity-level. Introducing a material reward for the referring member does not enhance that motivation — it reclassifies the act. The member goes from "I'm someone who helps people I care about" to "I'm someone who earns rewards for referrals." Those are meaningfully different self-concepts, and the first is more durable.

Model B also produces higher-quality referrals. A referring member motivated by identity selects the right person — someone who would genuinely benefit and likely succeed. A referring member motivated by a free month selects whoever is most likely to join, which is not the same person. The quality of the referred lead affects the entire downstream system: Jessica's nurture sequence converts higher-quality leads more efficiently, retention is better for referred members who arrived with genuine context, and the gym's community culture reinforces itself.

The enhanced PT intro session for the referred friend serves two purposes: it removes the intimidation of walking in without knowing what to do (the third-ranked objection for this avatar), and it creates a natural entry point for the PT upsell conversation. The comp session is not a giveaway — it is a structured introduction to the highest-margin service in the gym.

# SECTION 5: IMPLEMENTATION NOTE FOR KAI

The single most important thing you need to do to make this referral system work is not a technical setup. It is a team briefing.

Every trigger in this system depends on a staff member saying the right thing at the right moment without hesitation. Trigger 6 — the spontaneous comment — gives you a 90-second window. If your staff member has to think about what to say, the window closes. Trigger 3 — the coaching goal — requires your coach to transition from a moment of genuine pride into a referral ask without it feeling like a pivot to a sales script. That transition only works if the coach has said the words out loud at least twice before the moment arrives.

Before you launch any part of this system, sit with your team — coaches, front desk, anyone who talks to members — and role-play each script once. Not to perfect it. To make it feel like something a human would say. The scripts are short by design. The resistance will not be the length. The resistance will be the unfamiliarity of asking at all. One pass of "you be the member, I'll be the coach" removes most of that resistance.

The technical infrastructure — the GHL fields, the tracking links, the landing page — runs once it is built. The human layer runs only as well as your team has been prepared for it. That preparation takes thirty minutes and is the difference between a referral system that generates one or two new members a month and one that generates six.
